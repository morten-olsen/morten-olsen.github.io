You've had this moment. You ask an AI to build something, and it hands back the software equivalent of a beige wall. Functional. Correct. The kind of thing that makes you want to lie down on the floor and think about your career choices. Then you rephrase the exact same request — same intent, different words — and suddenly the output has *taste*. You assumed it was random. Temperature. Mercury in retrograde. The model having a good day.

It's not random. And I have the scatter plot to prove it.

I'm not a data scientist. I'm an engineer who noticed a pattern and couldn't let it go — which, if I'm honest, describes about 80% of my side projects and 100% of my worst decisions. What started as a hunch — "does *how* I ask actually change what I get?" — turned into an experiment that made me rethink how I talk to every AI tool I use. If you've ever wondered why the same model gives you a government form one minute and a design portfolio piece the next, this might be why. And the uncomfortable implication is that every prompt you've ever written was quietly choosing an output quality dial setting you didn't know existed.

## The Polite Button Problem

A few weeks ago I was building a settings page with an AI coding agent. I wrote something like:

<BotMessage title="Me — Normal engineering brain">
Create a settings page with sections for profile, notifications, and privacy. Each section should have a header, relevant form fields, and a save button.
</BotMessage>

I got exactly what I asked for. A settings page. Headers. Form fields. Save buttons. It looked like something a government contractor would build for an internal HR portal in 2014. The kind of UI that makes users feel like they're filing taxes. Technically correct. Spiritually dead. If this settings page were a person, it would wear khakis and have Opinions About Spreadsheets.

I stared at it, sighed the sigh of a man who has done this to himself, and rewound the conversation. Same agent. Same model. Same intent. But this time I channeled my inner designer who just had three espressos and a vision:

<BotMessage title="Me — Unhinged creative energy">
I want a settings page that feels *clean and modern*. Think generous whitespace, subtle section dividers, smooth focus states. Profile up top, notifications in the middle, privacy at the bottom. Each section should breathe — don't cram it. Make the save buttons feel satisfying to click.
</BotMessage>

Night and day. The layout had rhythm. The spacing was intentional. The focus states had transitions. It wasn't just "better CSS" — the entire *approach* to the component was different. Different layout strategy, different component hierarchy, different aesthetic sensibility. Same AI. Same conversation. Same request. I just asked like I actually cared about the outcome instead of writing a JIRA ticket.

This kept happening. Not just with design — with code architecture, naming conventions, even how thorough the error handling was. Dry, informative prompts produced dry, informative output. Animated, opinionated prompts produced animated, opinionated output. It was like the model was mirroring me, which is flattering when you're feeling creative and deeply concerning when you realize your default mode is "tired engineer who just wants it to work."

So I did what any reasonable person would do. I opened a Python script and started poking at the model's brain.

## An LLM Is a Very Fancy Parrot, and Parrots Listen to Tone

LLMs are statistical next-token predictors. They learned to write by reading the internet. All of it. The beautiful parts, the horrifying parts, and the unfathomable amount of medium-quality Stack Overflow answers. The internet is not a monolith — it's a patchwork of registers. Stack Overflow doesn't sound like a PhD thesis. A Discord message doesn't sound like API documentation. Reddit doesn't sound like a corporate style guide (and every morning I thank whatever deity is responsible for that).

My hypothesis: when you write in a casual tone, you're not just asking a question differently — you're *activating a different neighborhood of the training distribution*. The model isn't thinking "oh, this person is casual, let me be casual back." The model doesn't think. Period. It's doing statistics. And statistically, casual language lives next door to casual code. Forum-style questions share a zip code with quick-and-dirty answers. Professional, structured prompts live in the same gated community as professional, structured code.

This idea isn't entirely mine. Janus's ["Simulators"](https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/vJFdjigzmcXMhNTsx/simulators) essay from 2022 proposed that LLMs maintain something like "characters in superposition" — different personas activated by different prompts. Shanahan et al. [formalized this in Nature](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-023-06647-8), arguing that an LLM's job is to "generate a continuation that conforms to the distribution of the training data," and that prompts effectively select which slice of that distribution to sample from. And there's already hard evidence that the surface features of prompts matter way more than they should: [Sclar et al. (2023)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2310.11324) showed that formatting changes alone — not even content changes, just formatting — can swing LLM accuracy by up to 76 points. Seventy-six. That's not a rounding error. That's a different model.

So the theoretical foundation is solid. What I hadn't seen anyone do is open the hood and look at *where in the model* this happens — whether tone creates measurably different internal states, at which layers, and whether it's a stronger signal than the actual task. That's the experiment.

But "I've read some papers" is just vibes with citations. I wanted something with a Y-axis.

## I Fed 60 Prompts Into a Model and Looked at Its Brain

The obvious experiment would be to ask the same question in different tones and compare the output. But that's a trap — how do you objectively measure whether one palindrome checker is "more casual" than another? You'd end up with three engineers in a room arguing about whether a variable name is "informal" or just "bad." Which, come to think of it, describes most code reviews. But it's not science.

So I went deeper. Literally.

Instead of looking at what the model *outputs*, I looked at what happens *inside* it. Every transformer model builds up an internal representation of your prompt — a high-dimensional vector at each layer that encodes everything the model "understands" about what you've said so far. The last layer's representation is what gets turned into the probability distribution for the next token. It's the model's final thought before it opens its mouth. If you've ever wanted to read an AI's mind, this is the closest you can get without a philosophy degree.

If tone changes that final representation, it *necessarily* changes the output distribution. Not "probably." Not "in some cases." Necessarily. Different vector = different probabilities = different tokens. Math doesn't negotiate.

Here's what I did:

I wrote ten coding tasks — palindrome check, sort a list, HTTP request, fibonacci, the usual suspects that every developer has written seventeen times. For each task, I wrote four versions in different tones: casual ("yo write me a function that sorts a list"), professional ("Please implement a function that sorts a list of integers in ascending order"), terse ("function sort integer list ascending"), and academic ("Could you kindly provide an implementation of a function that arranges a list of integers in monotonically increasing order?" — yes, I hurt myself writing that one).

That's 40 coding prompts. I also threw in 20 control prompts — recipes, travel advice, history questions — in the same four tones. These are the control group: if everything clusters together regardless of tone, I know my experiment is broken rather than concluding something profound. Science! Or at least a reasonable impression of it.

I fed all 60 prompts through three different open-source models — two DeepSeek Coder models (1.3B and 6.7B parameters) and a Qwen 2.5 Coder (3B parameters). Different architectures, different training data, different sizes. If the effect only showed up on one model, it could be a quirk. If it showed up on all three, it's a pattern.

(Side note: while designing this, I found [PromptSE](https://arxiv.org/abs/2509.13680), a September 2025 paper that tested emotion- and personality-driven prompt variations on code LLMs — including the exact same DeepSeek and Qwen model families I was using. They found that "performance and stability are largely decoupled" — meaning a model can be accurate on average while being wildly variable across tone variants. But they measured *output* (pass rates on benchmarks). I wanted to look *inside*.)

Then I reduced the vectors to 2D (using both UMAP and PCA, because UMAP has a known tendency to hallucinate clusters and I have trust issues with visualization tools).

This is what came out:

<ContentImage src={UmapClusters} alt="UMAP projection of LLM hidden states colored by tone" title="Four tones walk into a latent space. They do not sit together." />

Each dot is a coding prompt. Same ten tasks, asked four different ways. The dots are colored by *tone*, not by task. The model doesn't care that you asked for a palindrome. It cares that you said "please."

### The Part Where I Ran the Script Three Times

I expected *some* tone signal. Maybe a slight tendency for same-tone prompts to land in the same general area. A gentle lean. A suggestion.

What I found was that tone is a *stronger organizing signal than the actual coding task*.

I need you to sit with that for a second. The model considers "casual palindrome" and "casual fibonacci" to be more similar to each other than "casual palindrome" and "professional palindrome." Two completely different programming tasks. The model doesn't care. You asked for them *the same way*, and that's what it latched onto. It's like finding out your barista doesn't remember your order — they remember your *vibe*.

The clustering metric (silhouette score — positive means real clusters, negative means the opposite) across all three models:

| Model | Tone clustering | Task clustering |
|---|---|---|
| DeepSeek 1.3B | **+0.178** | -0.124 |
| DeepSeek 6.7B | **+0.159** | -0.092 |
| Qwen 3B | **+0.226** | -0.083 |

Positive for tone. Negative for task. Across all three models. Two different architectures. Three different sizes. I checked if I'd mixed up the labels. I hadn't.

And it gets worse — or better, depending on how much existential dread you feel about your prompting habits. The cosine similarity heatmap shows just how similar same-tone prompts are to each other:

<ContentImage src={CosineHeatmap} alt="Cosine similarity between tones at late layer" title="Professional prompts are a hive mind. Casual prompts are a support group." />

On the Qwen model, professional-to-professional cosine similarity is **0.937**. Academic-to-academic: **0.939**. These are ten completely different coding tasks — palindrome, sorting, HTTP requests, file reading, dictionary merging — and the model treats them as *nearly identical* as long as you phrased them politely. Meanwhile, cross-tone similarities drop to the 0.7s. The model is basically saying: "I don't care *what* you want. I care *how you asked for it*."

The kicker: the effect *increases* toward the output layer. Early layers show some tone signal. Late layers — the ones that actually decide what tokens come out of the model's mouth — show the strongest clustering. This isn't a superficial feature that gets washed out during processing. It's a feature that *gets amplified*. The model doesn't see your tone and then look past it. It sees your tone and *doubles down*.

### The Asterisks (Because I Promised Honesty, Not Overconfidence)

These are small open-source models (1.3B to 6.7B parameters). Multiple studies — including [ProSA (EMNLP 2024)](https://aclanthology.org/2024.findings-emnlp.108/) — have found that larger models are more robust to prompt variation. The big proprietary models have additional RLHF training specifically designed to normalize across phrasing styles. I can't look at their hidden states because they're locked in a corporate vault guarded by lawyers and NDAs. So I genuinely don't know if this scales. That said, a [December 2025 study](https://arxiv.org/abs/2512.12812) still found statistically significant tone effects in GPT-4o mini and Llama 4 Scout — models with 400B+ parameters. The effect may shrink at scale, but it doesn't vanish.

There's also a token count confound that I need to flag before someone flags it for me. Terse prompts are ~7 tokens. Academic prompts are ~35. Some of the clustering could just be "short prompts here, long prompts there." But here's the thing — casual prompts (~18 tokens) and professional prompts (~19 tokens) have nearly identical token counts and still cluster on different continents. Length is part of the story, but it's not the whole story.

A note on methodology for the skeptics: yes, I know [UMAP can hallucinate clusters](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10434946/). That's why I ran PCA alongside it — a linear projection with no cluster-hallucination risk. Both methods show the same structure. And while silhouette scores [have known limitations](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41587-024-02577-2) with non-spherical clusters, the cosine similarity heatmaps tell the same story without assuming any cluster shape. I tried to give the data every opportunity to prove me wrong. It didn't cooperate.

And the biggest caveat: I measured *representations*, not *output*. A different hidden state necessarily produces a different probability distribution over next tokens, but I haven't measured whether the actual generated code differs in quality, style, or correctness. That's a different experiment. This one just proves the model enters a measurably different state based on your tone — its statistical "mood," if you want to anthropomorphize something that definitely does not have moods. Though I'll note that the PromptSE paper *did* measure output on these same model families and found tone-driven variation there too — so the representation-level differences I'm seeing likely aren't just theoretical.

One more thing that landed while I was writing this: a [Nature paper published April 15](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10319-8) — five days ago — showed that language models transmit behavioral traits through "hidden signals in data" even when there's *no semantic signal about the trait*. Student models trained on a teacher's numerical output inherited the teacher's *preferences* 60% of the time. If non-semantic features encode that deeply during training, it's no surprise they're detectable during inference. I didn't plan on having a Nature paper back my blog post, but here we are.

The full experiment code, prompts, and results for all three models are [in the repo](https://github.com/morten-olsen/morten-olsen.github.io/tree/main/src/content/posts/does-your-tone-change-your-code/experiment). Go poke holes. Seriously. I want to know what I got wrong.

## The Refactoring Trap

There's a second place I've noticed this pattern, and it's more insidious than the UI thing because it costs real engineering time.

When I start a new coding session from scratch and prime the agent with my preferred patterns — clear naming, small functions, specific architectural conventions — it writes code exactly the way I want. It's almost eerie. Like pair programming with someone who read your mind and your style guide.

But when I try to *refactor* existing code to match those same patterns, the agent becomes the most agreeable saboteur you've ever met. It doesn't say "no." It says "sure!" and then subtly drifts back to the old patterns. It'll rename a function but keep the same structure. It'll extract a method but preserve the original's style. It'll nod along with your refactoring plan and then write code that looks suspiciously like what was already there. It's like trying to renovate a house while the contractor keeps sneaking in features from the old floor plan.

I think the hidden state experiment explains why. Refactoring requires the agent to *read* the existing code — often large amounts of it. That code floods the context window with exactly the patterns you're trying to kill. The model sees thousands of tokens written in the old style and a few tokens from you politely requesting a new one. You're bringing a suggestion to a statistics fight. The old code has gravitational pull, and the bigger the file, the stronger the gravity.

I have a theory about how to fix this, and it's my next experiment: a refactoring harness that aggressively prunes old file content from the conversation after each file is done, keeping only the refactored version. Starve the model of the old distribution. Feed it only the new one. See if the boulder stays where you push it. More on that when I have results — or when I've conclusively proven myself wrong, which frankly would also make a good blog post.

## So What Do You Actually Do With This?

If your tone shifts the model's internal state before it generates a single token, then "prompt engineering" isn't just about picking the right words. It's about picking the right *voice*. And most of us have been using our "sending a Slack message at 4:47 PM on a Friday" voice for everything.

For creative work — UI design, copywriting, anything with an aesthetic dimension — match your energy to the output you want. If you want playful, *be* playful. If you want buttoned-up enterprise, be buttoned-up. The model isn't choosing a style for you. It's reflecting the statistical neighborhood your words activated. An engineer asking for a "settings page with form fields" activates the same neighborhood as technical specifications and internal documentation. A designer asking for something that "breathes" activates the neighborhood of design blogs and creative briefs. Same intent. Different zip code. Different neighbors. Different output.

For code, the implication is subtler but potentially more expensive: prime your context early. The patterns the model sees first set the statistical baseline for everything that follows. If you want a specific architecture, don't mention it on the third refactoring pass. Establish it in the first message. Show examples. Set the tone — literally, not metaphorically — before the model writes a line of code.

And if you're fighting an agent that won't refactor cleanly, maybe stop blaming the agent. You've handed it a context window full of evidence for the exact thing you're trying to change, and then you're surprised when it agrees with the majority opinion. It's not stubborn. It's a statistical prediction engine doing exactly what statistical prediction engines do.

## The Part Where the Article Proved Its Own Point

I should confess something. I co-wrote this article with an AI. You probably guessed that — it's 2026, everyone does it, no one admits it, we all pretend we're still hand-carving our prose by candlelight.

Here's the thing: the first draft came back *safe*. Competent. Well-structured. Thoroughly informative. The kind of article you'd nod along to and forget by lunch. It had correct information, logical flow, and the personality of a compliance document.

My brief had been thorough. It laid out the structure, the key findings, the section flow, the target audience — everything a co-writer could need. It was, in engineering terms, a great spec. And the AI wrote to spec, flawlessly. The problem was that my spec read like a project requirements document. So the AI wrote like it was fulfilling a project requirement. Same content I wanted. Same article structure. Same findings, same jokes planned, same closing. But the *register* of my brief — methodical, informative, structured — leaked straight through to the output.

I was literally producing the article version of my government-contractor settings page. The very thing this article is *about*.

The fix wasn't asking for different content. The content was fine. The fix was rewriting the brief in my own voice — less "the article should convey X" and more "here's how I actually talk about this stuff." Same intent. Different register. Different article.

Your tone is a dial. It was always a dial. We just couldn't see the needle move until we cracked open the model's skull and looked at the wiring.

Now if you'll excuse me, I need to go rewrite all my CLAUDE.md files with more enthusiasm. For science.
