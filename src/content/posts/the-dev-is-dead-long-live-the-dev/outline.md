# The Agentic Hangover: What We Broke, What We Build Next

## Working title options
- "The Agentic Hangover: What We Broke, What We Build Next"
- "Surviving the Vibe Coding Crash"
- "The Developer's Guide to Not Losing Your Mind in an Agentic World"

## Target length
3,000–3,500 words

## Thesis
We adopted agentic development faster than we developed the practices to use it well. Here's what's breaking, what we need to accept, and how to find your footing in a world that's shifting under you.

---

## Opening — Flash-forward + confession

Open with a moment where agentic development actually felt *right*. A recent win — maybe from the professional platform work or a GLaDOS session — where you and the AI were genuinely in sync, and you thought: "okay, *this* is what it's supposed to feel like."

Then rewind. "But it took breaking a lot of things to get here."

Establish the duality: by day, you're on a team reimagining an entire platform as AI-based agentic. By night, GLaDOS and personal experiments. You're making the bet *and* noticing the cracks. You're not writing this as a skeptic or an evangelist — you're writing it as someone in the middle of it, trying to be honest about both sides.

The flash-forward gives the reader a contract: *this isn't just a doom piece. There's somewhere good on the other side. Stay with me.*

## Section 1 — "We're Shipping Code Nobody Understands" → Comprehension checkpoints

### The problem: Comprehension debt
Experienced developers are measurably 19% slower with AI tools despite *predicting* they'd be 24% faster. Code churn is up 39% in AI-heavy projects. 76% of devs admit they don't fully understand code their AI wrote. We're building on foundations we can't inspect.

### The loss to grieve
The deep understanding you got from writing something yourself, line by line. The intuition for where bugs hide because you *built* the thing. That's atrophying. Name it honestly.

### The mitigation: Comprehension checkpoints
What's actually working: code review rituals adapted for AI output, "explain this back to me" sessions with the agent, mandatory human walkthroughs for critical paths. Specific practices, not platitudes. Draw from both professional and personal experience.

---

## Section 2 — "We Use Tools We Don't Trust" → Trust through verification

### The problem: The trust paradox
80% of developers use AI coding agents. Trust in AI accuracy has *dropped* from 40% to 29% year-over-year. We've never had a technology adopted this widely while confidence is actively declining. This is genuinely new in tech history.

### The loss to grieve
The confidence of knowing your tools are deterministic. `gcc` doesn't hallucinate. `npm install` doesn't improvise. There was a reliability contract with our toolchain that's been replaced by probabilistic output. That shift is unsettling, and pretending it's fine doesn't help.

### The mitigation: Trust through verification
Not "don't use AI" but building verification *into* the workflow. Concrete strategies: structured output validation, test-first workflows where the AI writes against your spec (not its own), guardrails that catch drift before it ships. The goal isn't blind trust or paranoid rejection — it's calibrated trust.

---

## Section 3 — "We're Cutting the Ladder Behind Us" → Reinventing apprenticeship

### The problem: The pipeline paradox
54% of engineering leaders plan to hire fewer juniors because of AI. But seniors who supervise AI are made by... being juniors first. We're cutting the pipeline that produces the people we need. 80,000 tech workers laid off in Q1 2026, 48% attributed to AI.

### The loss to grieve
The mentorship relationship. The slow, messy process of a junior growing into a senior through thousands of small mistakes. The shared struggle that builds team culture. AI can't replicate that, and if we automate away the entry point, we lose something real.

### The mitigation: Reinventing apprenticeship
New models: pair programming with AI as the third seat (junior learns *judgment* not just syntax), shifting junior roles from "write the boilerplate" to "verify and understand the AI's output," creating structured learning paths that use AI as a teaching tool rather than a replacement. What might this look like in practice?

## Section 4 — "The Commons Is Drowning" → Sustainable open source

### The problem: AI slop is DDoSing open source
cURL shut down its bug bounty — AI submissions tanked the valid rate to 5%. 20% of AI-suggested package dependencies don't exist (attackers are squatting those hallucinated names). The cost of generating plausible code is zero; the filtering cost falls entirely on unpaid maintainers.

### The loss to grieve
The open source ethos of "anyone can contribute." That democratic ideal is being weaponized by low-effort AI submissions. The barrier to contribution used to be *effort* — you had to care enough to understand the project. That natural filter is gone.

### The mitigation: Sustainable contribution models
Contribution gates that verify understanding, not just code correctness. Maintainer tooling that filters AI slop before it reaches human eyes. The uncomfortable but necessary conversation about whether "open to all contributions" is still viable, or whether we need new norms.

## Closing — "Finding the Fun Again"

Circle back to the flash-forward from the opening. That moment of sync wasn't an accident — it was the result of the practices described in the piece. The guardrails aren't restrictions; they're what make the collaboration actually *work*.

What does "craft" look like when the craft changes? Maybe it's in orchestration, in taste, in knowing *what* to build. Maybe it's in the things AI is still bad at — the weird, the novel, the deeply human judgment calls. The fun isn't gone; it moved.

End with something honest and open — a personal commitment, an unresolved question, an invitation. Not a summary. Not "in conclusion." Something that leaves the reader thinking.
