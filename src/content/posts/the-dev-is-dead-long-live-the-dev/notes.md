## The assembly-to-libraries analogy — expectations expand to fill the tooling

We used to write in assembly. A simple application took enormous time and precision craftsmanship. Then we got high-level languages. Then libraries. Then entire ecosystems. The application that once took weeks can today be recreated in minutes or hours — even without AI.

So did we conclude "software is solved"? Did we need fewer developers? No. The *expectations* of what software should do grew just as fast. The bar moved. Customers expected more features, more polish, more integrations, faster delivery. The companies and engineers who learned to leverage each wave of tooling advancement were the ones that thrived. The ones who clung to the old way didn't get replaced by the tools — they got outpaced by competitors who used them.

**The AI parallel:** AI is the next step in this same progression. It doesn't make software "done." It raises the bar for what's expected. The engineer who can leverage AI isn't doing less — they're doing *more*, at a higher level of ambition. The demand for software hasn't peaked. It's accelerating.

**Possible placement:**
- Strengthens the "Will I Lose My Job?" section — this is the historical counter-evidence to the doom narrative
- Could pair with the outsourcing parallel: outsourcing is the *economic* precedent, assembly-to-libraries is the *technical* precedent. Both point the same direction.
- Could also work in the evolution framing section as a more concrete, dev-specific example of "we've been here before"
- The key line: "We didn't need fewer developers when we got React. We needed developers who could think bigger."

## The evolution analogy

Human evolution is slow. Our technological progress has massively outpaced our biological rate of adaptation. We are, in a very real sense, living in a world we weren't built for. Our brains are wired for small tribes, immediate threats, and berry-picking — not for Slack notifications, global information overload, and sitting in chairs for eight hours.

But we've found ways to compensate. We use other human strengths — cooperation, tool-building, culture, institutions — to bridge the gap between what our biology can handle and what our environment demands. And in return, we've drastically improved our living standards, even though there are genuine tradeoffs compared to our hunter-gatherer days (loneliness, anxiety, sedentary health problems, etc.).

**The parallel to agentic development:** Our "developer instincts" evolved for a world where you wrote code, understood code, and maintained code yourself. The agentic shift has outpaced our ability to adapt our practices, habits, and mental models. We're the hunter-gatherer brain trying to operate in the industrial city. The answer isn't to go back to the savannah — it's to build the institutions, habits, and guardrails that let us thrive in the new environment, while being honest about what we lose in the trade.

- Could be the framing metaphor for the entire piece
- Could be a standalone section between the opening and the first problem/mitigation section — setting the philosophical frame
- Could be a single paragraph in the closing, tying it all together
- Could appear in the "grief" beats — we grieve the hunter-gatherer simplicity, but we don't actually want to go back

## "Will I lose my job?" — The outsourcing parallel

The honest answer is "maybe." But we've seen this movie before.

When outsourcing became big, every company saw the same thing: same hours, fraction of the price. So a lot of people lost their jobs. Companies pocketed the savings and their bottom lines looked great.

But quality was hard to control. And then the smart competitors realized something: instead of putting the savings on the bottom line, what if they *reinvested* that money in insourced labour? The in-house team — now freed from the less desirable grunt work handled by the outsourcing team — could focus entirely on high-value development. That converted back into topline revenue and generated more growth. The result: a downturn in jobs, followed by an actual uptick. Not the same jobs — but more jobs, and often better ones.

**The AI parallel:** The same cycle is likely playing out. Companies *can* dramatically cut engineering headcount and just pay for AI tooling. But here's the thing — if your entire engineering output can be replicated by a competitor with a Claude Code license and a weekend, then your engineering team wasn't your moat in the first place. You've just made yourself trivially disruptable.

The companies that win won't be the ones that fire engineers to save money. They'll be the ones that reinvest the productivity gains: let the AI handle the commodity work, and redirect human engineers toward the things that are actually hard to replicate — domain expertise, product judgment, architectural vision, customer understanding. The stuff that *is* the moat.

**The uncomfortable middle:** There will genuinely be a downturn period. Some jobs will go away. That's real, and pretending otherwise is dishonest. But the equilibrium point is probably *more* engineering jobs, not fewer — just different ones, focused higher up the value chain.

**Possible uses:**
- Could be a standalone section, maybe between the pipeline/junior developer problem and the mitigations
- Pairs well with the evolution analogy: same pattern — disruption, adaptation, new equilibrium, genuine losses along the way
- The "disruptable in a weekend" line is punchy enough to be a pull quote
- Needs to be delivered with real honesty about the downturn period — no glossing over the pain with "it'll all work out"

**Key insight — don't give a list, give the habit:** Tempting to list specific "AI-proof" skills, but any such list has a shelf life. The real advice is: make it a *practice* to continuously track where the frontier is. What can AI not solve *yet*? That "yet" moves. The developer who keeps up with that moving line — who always knows what the current gap is and positions themselves in it — is the one you keep. They're the employee who generates the topline your competitor with just AI tooling can't create. The advice isn't "learn X skill." It's "never stop tracking what's still hard."

**TONE WARNING — don't induce anxiety!** The "track the frontier or get left behind" framing can easily read as stressful and exhausting. We need to land this without making people feel like they're on a treadmill that's speeding up. 

**The better framing — make AI your partner:** Instead of positioning this as a defensive survival strategy, flip it. The best way to understand where you add value is to actually *collaborate* with AI day to day. When you make it your partner:
- You outsource the grunt work you never liked doing anyway
- You deliver results faster (provided you learn to quality-control the output)
- And — almost as a side effect — you develop a natural, intuitive sense for what *you* bring to the collaboration that the AI can't

That last point is the important one. You don't need to anxiously study the frontier from the outside. By working *with* AI, the frontier becomes obvious from the inside. You feel it every day: "this part, I needed to do. That part, it handled fine." Over time, that gives you a very clear picture of your own value — and that value is likely what pays your salary going forward.

**So the message isn't** "constantly monitor what AI can do or you'll be obsolete." **It's** "partner with it, and you'll naturally understand both where it helps and where you're irreplaceable." One is anxious. The other is empowering.

**Address the doom narrative directly in the piece.** Don't just avoid inducing anxiety — actively disarm the anxiety the reader already has. Many readers will arrive carrying stress from the flood of "AI will replace developers" articles. Acknowledge that head-on: "You've probably read a dozen articles telling you to be scared. I want to offer you a different mental model."

Then replace it. The shift we want to take the reader on:
- **From:** anxious, defensive, clinging to the old way, dreading obsolescence
- **To:** excited, curious, experimenting with AI as a partner, seeing their own value clearly

The key is that this isn't toxic positivity or denial. We've already been honest about the real losses and the real downturn. The reader trusts us *because* we didn't sugarcoat. So when we then say "but here's why I think you can put the anxiety down" — it lands differently than if we'd led with it.

This could be a turning point in the piece — maybe the bridge between the problem/grief sections and the closing. A moment where the tone deliberately shifts. Something like: "If you've been reading the doom articles and feeling a knot in your stomach — I get it. I've felt it too. But I want to offer you a trade: swap the anxiety for curiosity. Not because the risks aren't real, but because curiosity is the thing that will actually help you navigate them. Anxiety just makes you freeze."

**Goal:** reader finishes the piece feeling energized and ready to experiment, not burned out or scared. Drive, not dread.
