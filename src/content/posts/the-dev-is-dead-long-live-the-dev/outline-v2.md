# The Agentic Hangover: What We Broke, What We Build Next

## Target length
3,000–3,500 words

## Thesis
We adopted agentic development faster than we developed the practices to use it well. Here's what's breaking, what we're losing, and why I think the other side is actually worth being excited about — if we steer toward it intentionally.

## Emotional arc
**Anxious → honest → relieved → excited.** The reader likely arrives carrying doom-article stress. We earn trust by being honest about the problems, then actively disarm the anxiety and replace it with a better mental model. They should close the tab feeling energized, not burned out.

## Epistemic stance
The author doesn't have the answers. He has suggestions, field notes, and bets. In some areas he's a little ahead in the story, in some maybe behind. This is projection, not prediction — "here's where I think this could end up if we try to steer it there."

Establish credibility through duality: by day, building an AI-based agentic platform professionally. By night, GLaDOS and personal experiments. All in on the bet, eyes open to the cracks.

Then: "But getting here required stumbling through a version that very much didn't work." Honest disclaimer — I don't have the answers, I have suggestions. Some tested, some still testing, some just bets. Like everyone else, watching this unfold in real time.

The rewind sets up the journey. The reader knows there's green grass at the end because they just saw it.

## The frame — We've outrun our instincts

Short section (2-3 paragraphs). The evolution analogy, delivered with self-awareness.

Human evolution is slow. Our tech progress outpaced our biology long ago. We live in a world our brains weren't built for — wired for small tribes and berry-picking, operating in a landscape of Slack notifications and global information overload. But we compensated. We used other human strengths — cooperation, culture, institutions — to bridge the gap. Our living standards improved enormously, even though there are real tradeoffs compared to hunter-gatherer days.

Same thing is happening to developers right now. Our "developer instincts" evolved for writing, understanding, and maintaining code yourself. The agentic shift has outpaced our practices. We're the hunter-gatherer brain in the industrial city. The answer isn't going back to the savannah — it's building the habits and guardrails for the new environment, while being honest about the trade.

This sets the philosophical frame for everything that follows: disruption → honest loss → adaptation → new equilibrium.

## Section 1 — "We're Shipping Code Nobody Understands"

**The problem:** Comprehension debt. Devs are 19% slower with AI despite predicting 24% faster. Code churn up 39%. 76% admit they don't fully understand AI-written code. We're building on foundations we can't inspect.

**The honest loss:** The deep intuition from writing it yourself. The sense for where bugs hide because you *built* the thing. That's atrophying. Don't pretend it's fine.

**What I'm trying:** Comprehension checkpoints. Code review rituals adapted for AI output. "Explain this back to me" sessions with the agent. Human walkthroughs for critical paths. Not platitudes — specific practices from professional and personal experience.

---

## Section 2 — "We Use Tools We Don't Trust"

**The problem:** The trust paradox. 80% adoption, trust *down* from 40% to 29%. Mass adoption + declining confidence = genuinely unprecedented in tech history.

**The honest loss:** Deterministic tooling. `gcc` doesn't hallucinate. `npm install` doesn't improvise. The reliability contract with our toolchain has been replaced by probabilistic output.

**What I'm trying:** Trust through verification. Test-first workflows where AI writes against *your* spec. Structured validation. Guardrails that catch drift before it ships. The goal: calibrated trust — not blind faith, not paranoid rejection.

---

## Section 3 — "We're Cutting the Ladder Behind Us"

**The problem:** The pipeline paradox. 54% of leaders plan to hire fewer juniors. But seniors are made from juniors. 80,000 tech workers laid off Q1 2026, 48% attributed to AI.

**The honest loss:** The mentorship relationship. The slow, messy growth through thousands of small mistakes. The shared struggle that builds team culture. If we automate away the entry point, we lose something real.

**What I'm trying:** Reinventing apprenticeship. AI as the third seat in pair programming. Junior roles shift from "write the boilerplate" to "verify and understand the output." Structured learning that uses AI as teaching tool, not replacement.

---

## Section 4 — "The Commons Is Drowning"

**The problem:** AI slop DDoSing open source. cURL's bug bounty killed — valid rate at 5%. 20% of AI-suggested dependencies don't exist; attackers squat the hallucinated names. Zero cost to generate plausible code; filtering cost falls on unpaid maintainers.

**The honest loss:** The "anyone can contribute" ethos. Effort used to be the natural filter — you had to care enough to understand the project. That barrier is gone.

**What I'm trying:** Sustainable contribution models. Gates that verify understanding, not just code correctness. Maintainer tooling that filters before human review. The uncomfortable conversation about whether "open to all" is still viable.

## The turn — "Will I lose my job?" and why the doom narrative is wrong

This is the pivot point where the tone shifts. Address the anxiety head-on.

**Acknowledge it:** "You've probably read a dozen articles telling you to be scared. I've felt that knot too."

**The outsourcing parallel:** We've seen this movie. Outsourcing promised same output for a fraction of the cost. Jobs were lost. Bottom lines looked great. But quality was hard to control, and the smart competitors realized: reinvest the savings in insourced talent focused on high-value work → convert back to topline revenue and growth. Downturn, then uptick. Not the same jobs — better ones.

**The AI version:** If a company fires its engineers and just pays for AI tooling, and their output can be replicated by a competitor with a Claude Code license and a weekend — their engineering was never their moat. They've made themselves trivially disruptable. The winners reinvest productivity gains into human engineers doing the work that's hard to replicate.

**The honest middle:** Yes, there will be a downturn period. Some jobs will go away. Don't pretend otherwise. But the equilibrium is likely more engineering jobs, not fewer — different, higher up the value chain.

**Then disarm the anxiety.** Don't give a list of "AI-proof skills" — any list has a shelf life. Instead, offer the better mental model:

**Make AI your partner.** When you collaborate with AI daily:
- You outsource grunt work you never liked anyway
- You deliver faster (if you learn to quality-control the output)
- And almost as a side effect, you develop a natural sense for what *you* bring that the AI can't

You don't need to anxiously study the frontier from the outside. By working *with* AI, the frontier becomes obvious from the inside. You feel it every day: "this part, I needed to do. That part, it handled." That gives you a clear picture of your own value — the value that pays your salary.

**The emotional shift:** "Swap the anxiety for curiosity. Not because the risks aren't real, but because curiosity is the thing that actually helps you navigate them. Anxiety just makes you freeze."

## Closing — "Finding the Fun Again"

Circle back to the opening. That moment of sync wasn't luck — it was the result of the practices and the mindset described in the piece. The guardrails aren't restrictions; they're what make the collaboration work.

The fun isn't gone. It moved. Craft now lives in orchestration, in taste, in knowing *what* to build, in the judgment calls AI still can't make. And by partnering with it rather than fighting it or fearing it, you find that naturally.

End with something honest and open. Not a summary. A personal commitment, an unresolved question, an invitation. Leave the reader thinking — and ideally, ready to open their laptop and try something.
